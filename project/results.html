Results

Result-CamR biosensor


       In order to construct a borneol biosensor in Yarrowia lipolytica, it is necessary to express the borneol-induced reporter gene. In this study, we used the allosteric transcription factor CamR manipulation system of Pseudomonas to heterologously express the inhibitory operon CamR in Yarrowia lipolytica. CamR binding sequence was inserted between the TATA box and the transcription start site ( TSS ) of the endogenous strong promoter pTEF1 promoter, then the promoter was transformed into a borneol-inducible promoter. The hrGFP was selected as the reporter gene, and the initiation efficiency of the promoter was characterized by the fluorescence intensity per OD600 ( RFU / OD600 ). In the absence of borneol, CamR protein binds to the insertion sequence of the promoter, inhibits the transcription of the downstream hrGFP gene, and the yeast does not emit fluorescence or just emits weak fluorescence ( off state ) ; in the presence of borneol in the environment, CamR binds to borneol and undergoes allosteric changes, and cannot bind to the insertion sequence in the promoter, thereby relieving the inhibition of reporter gene expression. In that case, promoter normally initiates transcription, and the yeast emits green fluorescence (on state ). The induction effect of borneol on the expression of reporter gene was positively correlated with the concentration of borneol in a certain range, so the concentration of borneol contained in yeast culture could be reflected by the fluorescence intensity of yeast.

       Due to the complex promoter structure of yeast, the insertion position of CamR binding sequence may have a great influence on the induction efficiency of the sensor. We scanned the insertion site of the 48 bp sequence between the TATA box and the transcription start site, and inserted the CamR binding sequence step by step with 6 bp length to form eight promoters with different CamR binding sequence insertion positions, and tested their induction efficiency at different borneol concentrations.

       After preliminary experiments, it was found that the constructed promoters with the 5th to the 8th insertion sites had no induction activity, that is, there was no difference in fluorescence intensity before and after borneol induction. Therefore, the yeast containing the inducible promoter of the first to fourth insertion sites was cultured, and the expression of hrGFP was induced using a medium containing 0 mM, 0.5 mM, 1 mM, and 2 mM borneol. The yeast containing the inducible promoter of the fifth insertion site was cultured as a negative control. After 22 hours of culture, the relative fluorescence intensity ( RFU ) and OD600 were detected by microplate reader, and the ratio of RFU/OD600 was the expression intensity of yeast green fluorescent protein. In the induction experiment, the second, third and fourth insertion positions made the promoter show a significant borneol induction effect, and the second and third insertion positions showed a good positive correlation with the concentration of borneol in a certain concentration range.

       Notably, without the addition of borneol, each group showed a certain fluorescence intensity, indicating that the binding of CamR to the pTEF1 promoter could not completely inhibit the expression of the reporter gene, and there was still a certain leakage. In addition, the insertion of the CamR binding sequence may destroy part of the promoter structure, resulting in a decrease in the strength of the pTEF1 promoter.

Result-microbial cell factory


       We selected the monoterpenes nerol, borneol and linalool as the target products of the cell factory. The mevalonate ( MVA ) pathway is the main pathway for the formation of monoterpene precursor GPP or nerolyl pyrophosphate ( NPP ). The MVA pathway is endogenous in the Y.lipolytica PO1f strain [ 6 ]. However, PO1f does not have the ability to synthesize various monoterpene secondary metabolites under natural conditions. Therefore, we transferred the exogenous monoterpene synthase gene into the PO1f strain. After expression, the precursor GPP / NPP produced by the MVA pathway is converted into the above three monoterpene products. The monoterpene synthases used in this study were all enzymes with high and stable yield of microbial production reported in the past.

       For nerol, its synthesis requires NPP as a precursor, and the synthesis pathway involves a two-step reaction of DMAPP and IPP to generate NPP and NPP dephosphorylation to generate nerol. The first step was catalyzed by NPP synthase ( SlNPPS ) from Solanum lycopersicum, and the second step was catalyzed by nerol synthase LdtBPPSF162A. We constructed the pHUH-LdtBPPS-NPPS plasmid, and transformed it into PO1f strain to obtain nerol production strain PLN1.

       For borneol and linalool, their synthesis depends on GPP as a precursor. In the metabolic process of the MVA pathway, the bifunctional enzyme ERG20 is responsible for catalyzing the synthesis of GPP and farnesyl pyrophosphate ( FPP ). In order to increase the concentration of intracellular GPP, we mutated ERG20 to produce double mutations of F88W and N119W by site-directed mutagenesis, reducing the activity of FPP synthase, thereby increasing the supply of monoterpene precursor GPP. We constructed the pHUH-BbTPS3-ERG20 and pHUH-AaLINS-ERG20 plasmids by using the BbTPS3 gene and the AaLINS gene with the ERG20F88WN119W, and transferred them into the PO1f strain to obtain the borneol production strain PBE1 and the linalool production strain PAE1.

       After fermentation, we detected the concentration of the borneol in the culture of PBE1 by GC-MS. The molecular weight of borneol is 154. By searching the MS fragments of all elution time, there was no molecular ion peak of relative molecular weight 154 in the fermentation product of PBE1, indicating that borneol was not detected, and the possible elution peak in the GC result was not the target product.

Introduction-Yarrowia and CamR biosensor


       Yarrowia lipolytica has a unique advantage in the production of terpenoid natural products. Y.lipolytica has a high metabolic flux of acetyl-CoA and NAD ( P ) H, which can be processed by the endogenous mevalonate ( MVA ) pathway into a large amount of geranyl pyrophosphate ( GPP ), the common precursor of monoterpene synthesis. Moreover, there are oil droplets that accumulate a large amount of lipids in Yarrowia lipolytica, which can store these lipophilic terpenoids to reduce their cytotoxicity to the engineering yeast. For the synthesis of heterologous monoterpenes, a variety of plant-derived monoterpene synthase gene have been isolated and identified. We chose three synthases with heterologous expression stability and high yield for the production of borneol, linalool and nerol.

       On the other hand, the detection of monoterpenes is often dependent on gas chromatography-mass spectrometry ( GC-MS ), which is expensive and low-throughput, and cannot meet the requirements of screening high-yield strains. In order to achieve endogenous high-throughput detection of monoterpenes, the construction of biosensors is one of the common strategies. In 2022, d 'Oelsnitz et al. reported an operon that can be specifically induced by borneol, which was evolved from operon Pcamr of Pseudomonas. It is a high-potential element for constructing the borneol biosensor.

Discussion-Biosensor


       At present, our sensor is still not sensitive enough to detect only the presence of higher concentrations of borneol, that is, its detection limit is too high. The production of monoterpenes in microbial cell factories is still at the microgram level, and its production is difficult to be reflected by our detectors. We believe that the reason may be the presence of operon leakage, the insertion of the manipulation sequence may destroy the promoter or the insertion site is not the best. In order to solve these problems, increasing the copy number of the operator sequence and enhancing the expression of CamR can effectively prevent the leakage of the operon, and reducing the screening step length to 1bp may be able to screen out the CamR insertion site with higher induction efficiency.

       Moreover, there is significant room for improvement in the construction of our yeast cell factory. In the future, we plan to optimize the enzyme ratios in the metabolic pathway to achieve higher metabolic flux, enhancing substrate utilization efficiency and increasing final product yield, thereby approaching the detection range of biosensors. Additionally, the directed evolution system based on EvolvR still faces some challenging issues, such as undetectable off-target effects, limited mutability despite strong specificity, and difficulties in expressing and correctly folding due to large protein molecules. However, we have reason to believe that the continuous directed evolution system still holds unparalleled prospects. In the near future, many of these problems are expected to be effectively resolved. However, due to time constraints, we have not yet successfully tested the effectiveness of the continuous directed evolution system, nyEvolvR, in the production chassis. Moving forward, we aim to further evaluate the effect of the directed evolution system for specific synthetic enzymes and compare it with rational design, ultimately establishing an unconventional yeast platform that integrates production, endogenous detection, and directed evolution.

       During the exploration process, we encountered various problems that were properly resolved through our continuous adjustments. For example, the fermentation conditions required some trial and error, consuming a significant amount of time and cost. We also explored the conditions for yeast transformation efficiency and colony PCR validation methods, in order to efficiently screen positive clones. However, there are still some issues that we have not yet solved, such as the complexity of the EcN genome composition, making it difficult to obtain fragments of expected length via PCR, and the low efficiency of yeast co-transformation. Through the research and exploration process, we have learnt many things and gained a lot of insights. We will continue to explore and seek advice and contribute our humble efforts to directed evolution.
